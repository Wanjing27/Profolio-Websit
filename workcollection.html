Pain is something that anyone can feel. From accidently hitting a toe on the corner of a table or couch to neck pain from sleeping the wrong way; it is a universal concept. However, the pain one feels from heartbreak is something unique and, at times, indescribable. This is a story, based off of the song Eight by IU, about someone experiencing that pain. A recent break up has occurred. After days of sorrow, the main character finds themself casually scrolling through their instagram feed as a distraction from their pain. Everything was going well until something appeared on their feed. It was a post her ex had with his new girlfriend. They looked so happy. “Are you happy now? Finally happy now?” she asked her phone thinking he could hear her. In that moment, the main character felt like they lost everything. The one person she loved, relied on, trusted, and shared most of their life with, is gone. The person who would make them coffee in the morning, rub their back when having trouble sleeping, holding them after a nightmare, and going on random late night drives to Walmart, is gone. The love of her life leaving, made her realize that she never wants to love if this was the outcome. Feeling the large amount of pain, she wanted to travel through the beautiful, old and worn out memories they once shared. Going to her own instagram page where she did not delete any couple pictures, she scrolled down pretending he was still there. The first post she saw made her remember them dancing under the orange sun having the best day of their life. She realized that no separation was meant to be, but she wanted to continue to meet him at the memories that were once beautiful. She wished they could be forever young. Then it appeared: their anniversary picture. He went from saying he'll stay with her forever to a stranger. “If I'm in this kind of nightmare. I promise I won't wake up ever again,” she said to herself. That's when she realized the only warm thing left in her life was her bed and he was not coming back.

Henriette

Henriette was created around the year of 2012; its sole purpose was to standardize the street signs across the city. The typeface was available in both Heavy and Bold Condensed version in order to support short street names as well as longer ones. Over the years, Henriette has been adopted and redrawn by several enamel factories. Now, it is mostly used as headlines and logotypes for magazines and news websites due to its Roman and Italic weights. Each offers the same character set and features throughout the width spectrum: normal, condensed, compressed. This gives even more flexibility when it comes to fitting a headline or creating a logotype.

This experimental foray into artistic expression celebrates the marriage of existing skills and the acquisition of new ones. Each element of design plays in harmony, leading the eye through a dance of discovery. The concealed letters and numbers bestow an enigmatic allure, transforming the piece into a captivating riddle for the viewer to decode.

Embedded within the tapestry of typographic history, this piece becomes a vivid portrayal of the intricate dance between letters and meaning. Each typographic gem from the annals of time finds renewed vibrancy, as their unique forms intertwine to narrate the evolution of communication through the ages.

Typeface can be defined as "a group of characters, letters and numbers that share the same design." Different designs include variations in size, weight, slope, width, etc. and each variation represents a font. A font is a "specific style of typeface with a set width, size, and weight". Garamond, Times and Arial are examples of typefaces, and some examples of different fonts include 16pt Arial Bold and italic. There are new typefaces being developed everyday.

The Wolrd Best Buttermilk

Words, the symphonies of human thought, find their visual serenade within this art piece. With meticulous detail infused into each curve, hue, and nuance, the word "Buttermilk" transforms into a visual melody. This creation seeks to evoke a visceral response, inviting the audience to not only perceive the word but to experience its essence through a symphony of color and form.

Buttermilk is an elegant font with a flow between each and every character due to its extensive array of ligatures from automatic programs. Along with it's great drop-caps and fancy numerals, it easily becomes the star of the show in any project with the special feature of curvy and heavily circular terminal serifs and it's huge contrast of the thick downstrokes and the thin horizontal stroke. Together with it's unique elegant feet that pound terminals similar to a floral pattern, it has a feminine feel to it. This font does not have an italic type due to the fact that it's stroke already has an 60-70 degree italic angle. Unlike other modern style serif typefaces, Buttermilk's thick vertical and thin horizontal strokes create a distinctive smooth character seamlessly connected from one end to another like liquid cream. This is why it is easy to arrange it beautifully and why Hische named it Buttermilk. However, Hische's original reason for naming this font Buttermilk was because she wanted to use the tt ligature.
